Towards How Continuous Integration Bad Practices Affect Software Health

This website contains complementary data to the submitted paper.

Abstract

Continuous Integration (CI) is a modern software engineering practice widely used in both closed-source and open-source projects. Especially in immature software development environments, CI failures can occur that will damage the quality of the software, called CI bad practices. CI's goal is to improve the software quality and, at the same time, promote faster deliveries. However, the negative impacts that CI bad practices can cause to the internal quality attributes of the software (e.g., cohesion, coupling, complexity, size and inheritance), are not yet known. In this sense, we conducted an exploratory study on closed-source projects to investigate how CI bad practices can affect software health. We observe (i) the impact on the quality internal attributes after the implantation of CI, and (ii) the CI bad practices that most affect the internal quality attributes according to the perspective of the software teams. Our study is based on quantitative data carried out based on data collected from 5 closed-source systems developed by our industrial partners. Our findings indicate that: (i) CI adoption does not necessarily imply improvements in software health, (ii) CI bad practices can negatively impact the internal quality attributes of closed-source systems, and (iii) the culture of the development environment can be crucial to the success of CI in projects.
